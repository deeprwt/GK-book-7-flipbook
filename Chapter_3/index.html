These jaw-dropping facts about the oceans and marine life will definitely leave you stunned and in awe!

Life under water began 3.1 billion years ago. In comparison, creatures on land only appeared 400 million years ago!

Hundreds of marine creatures thrive in the deepest part of the ocean called the Mariana Trench. This trench is around 36,000 feet deep in the Pacific Ocean.

Some of the smallest marine organisms can cure the biggest diseases. The blue-green algae have been approved by the National Cancer Institute in USA for the treatment of some types of cancer.

Green turtles, mostly found in the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, migrate up to 2,600 km from their habitat to lay their eggs on the beaches of Southeast Asia, India, the Western Pacific, and Central America.

Jellyfish have no heart, brain, mouth, eyes, or lungs. They have been around for 650 million years. It means they were around even before the dinosaurs! Some varieties of jellyfish even glow in the dark.

Snakes aren't just found on land. The sea snake is found in the warm waters of the Indian and Pacific oceans and usually feeds on small fish. A relative of the cobra, it has over sixty different varieties and is highly poisonous.

3. Marine life

From a biological perspective, corals are tiny immobile animals called polyps, which have a hard shell. Coral reefs take thousands of years to form, and are home to many fish, sea animals, and aquatic plants.

A type of fish called parrotfish covers itself in a thick layer of mucus at night to avoid being smelled out by predators while it is asleep!

Most starfish have five arms. But some species can have anywhere between 20 and 40 arms.

Squids have the least violent defence mechanism. When under attack, they simply release a dark ink that makes the water cloudy. This reduces the predator's vision and enables the squid to escape!

The waterwheel plant is a marine plant that is carnivorous in nature. It is a free-floating, rootless plant that is usually found on the surface of the oceans. While stunning to look at, it snaps its leaves shut to trap its prey.

Phytoplankton, a unicellular plant, plays an integral role in the environment. It is the foundation of the marine food web, acting as a source of food for multiple forms of marine life. The billions of phytoplankton present in the ocean absorb carbon dioxide, balancing the composition of the atmosphere.
